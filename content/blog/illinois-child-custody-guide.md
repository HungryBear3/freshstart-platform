Navigating child custody in Illinois can feel overwhelming — especially during an already emotional time. Illinois courts prioritize a child's best interests when awarding custody, evaluating each parent's relationship with the child, stability, and ability to provide a safe home.

## Types of Custody in Illinois

Illinois recognizes two dimensions of custody:

- **Legal Custody:** Decision-making authority over major life decisions — education, healthcare, religious upbringing. Can be awarded jointly (both parents share decisions) or solely (one parent decides).
- **Physical Custody (Parenting Time):** Where the child lives day-to-day. Courts often approve joint arrangements to keep both parents involved, but sole physical custody is possible when circumstances require it.

## How Courts Decide

Illinois uses a "best interests of the child" standard. Judges consider:

1. Each parent's relationship and involvement with the child
2. The child's adjustment to home, school, and community
3. The wishes of the child (if old enough to express a mature preference)
4. History of cooperation — or conflict — between the parents
5. Any history of domestic violence or substance abuse

## Creating a Parenting Plan

Illinois requires divorcing parents to submit a parenting plan — a document outlining parenting time schedules, holiday responsibilities, and decision-making processes. Well-drafted plans reduce future disputes and are more likely to be approved as-is by the court.

Key items to include:
- Regular parenting time schedule (weekdays, weekends, overnights)
- Holiday and school break rotation
- Communication protocols between parents
- Process for resolving disagreements

## Modifying Custody Orders

Life changes. If there's a significant change in circumstances — a relocation, job loss, safety concern, or shift in the child's needs — either parent can petition the court to modify existing custody or parenting time orders.
