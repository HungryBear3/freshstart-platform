Hiring a divorce attorney in Illinois costs an average of $10,000–$25,000 per spouse for a contested case. But if you and your spouse are in agreement on the key issues, you may be able to handle the entire process yourself — what's called a **pro se divorce**.

Here's what you need to know.

## Who Can File Pro Se?

Pro se (representing yourself) works best when:

- The divorce is **uncontested** — you and your spouse agree on property, debts, and any parenting arrangements
- You don't have complex financial assets (multiple properties, business ownership, significant pension division)
- Communication with your spouse is civil enough to sign documents together
- Neither party has a history of domestic violence or a significant power imbalance

If your case involves contested custody, a business valuation, a pension requiring a QDRO, or significant financial complexity, an attorney's involvement is worth the cost. But for straightforward uncontested divorces, pro se is absolutely viable.

## Illinois Requirements

Before you file, confirm:

**Residency:** At least one spouse must have lived in Illinois for **90 days** before filing.

**Grounds:** Illinois is a no-fault state. You only need to allege "irreconcilable differences" — no need to prove fault or blame.

**Separation:** Courts require that you've been living separate and apart for at least **6 months** before a divorce can be granted. This can sometimes overlap with the time your case is pending.

## The Pro Se Process, Step by Step

**Step 1: Reach a full agreement**
Before you file anything, get agreement in writing (or at minimum a clear verbal understanding) on:
- Division of all property and debts
- Whether either spouse receives maintenance (alimony)
- Parenting plan and child support (if applicable)

Trying to file before you've truly agreed leads to a contested case.

**Step 2: Prepare your court forms**
Illinois uses standardized court forms. You'll need at minimum:
- Petition for Dissolution of Marriage
- Marital Settlement Agreement
- Financial Affidavit (both spouses)
- If children: Joint Parenting Agreement and Child Support Worksheet
- Judgment for Dissolution of Marriage

Forms are available at the Illinois Courts website or your county circuit court clerk's office. They must be completed accurately and completely — errors cause delays.

**Step 3: File with the circuit court**
File your Petition for Dissolution (and any accompanying forms) with the Circuit Court Clerk in the county where you or your spouse lives. Pay the filing fee ($289–$337 in Cook County; less in other counties) or request a fee waiver if you qualify.

**Step 4: Serve your spouse (or get a waiver)**
Your spouse must be formally notified of the filing. In an uncontested divorce, this is usually handled with a **Waiver of Service** — your spouse simply signs a document confirming they received the papers and don't require formal service.

**Step 5: Wait out the separation period**
If you haven't already been separated for 6 months, you'll need to wait. The clock typically starts from when you and your spouse began living as a separated couple.

**Step 6: File remaining documents and request a hearing**
Once agreements are finalized and the separation period is satisfied, file your Marital Settlement Agreement, parenting plan, and other supporting documents. The court will schedule a brief hearing (often 10–15 minutes) where a judge reviews the paperwork and grants the dissolution.

**Step 7: Get your Judgment of Dissolution**
The judge signs the final order. Your divorce is finalized. You'll get a certified copy for your records.

## Common Mistakes That Cause Delays

- **Incomplete financial affidavit** — courts return these if any section is blank
- **Parenting plan that doesn't meet Illinois requirements** — the statute requires specific provisions; missing them means rewriting
- **Wrong forms for your county** — Cook County has slightly different forms than downstate
- **Not accounting for the 6-month separation** — some couples try to finalize before they're legally eligible
- **Forgetting to address a debt or asset** — anything left out of the settlement agreement can create problems later

## When You Do Need a Lawyer

Even if you start pro se, consult an attorney if:
- Your spouse hires one (you're suddenly at a disadvantage)
- You discover a pension or retirement account that needs a QDRO to divide
- Child custody becomes disputed
- You own a business together
- There are allegations of hidden assets

A one-time consultation ($150–300/hour) is still far cheaper than full representation.
