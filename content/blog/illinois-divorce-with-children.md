Divorcing with children adds significant complexity to the process. Illinois courts prioritize the best interests of the child above all else — and the legal framework around parenting reflects that.

Here's what every parent going through an Illinois divorce needs to understand.

## "Custody" Is Now "Parental Responsibilities"

Illinois eliminated the terms "custody" and "visitation" from family law in 2016. Instead, Illinois law uses:

- **Allocation of parental responsibilities** — who makes major decisions for the child
- **Parenting time** — the schedule of when each parent has the child

This isn't just a semantic change. The framework emphasizes both parents' involvement rather than one parent "winning custody."

## Decision-Making Responsibilities

Parental responsibilities are divided into four categories:

1. **Education** — school enrollment, special education decisions
2. **Healthcare** — medical, dental, mental health treatment
3. **Religion** — religious upbringing and activities
4. **Extracurricular activities** — sports, clubs, enrichment programs

These can be allocated to one parent (sole) or both parents (joint). Joint responsibility doesn't mean equal time — it means both parents have a say in major decisions.

## Parenting Time

Parenting time is the actual schedule. Common arrangements:

**Equal/50-50:** Week on/week off, or 2-2-3 rotation. Works best when both parents live close to the child's school.

**Primary with regular visitation:** Child lives primarily with one parent. The other parent has parenting time on alternating weekends plus one or two weeknights per week.

**Other arrangements:** Courts can approve any arrangement both parents agree to, as long as it serves the child's best interests.

## Child Support in Illinois

Illinois uses an **income shares model** — both parents' incomes factor into the calculation. The basic formula:

1. Calculate both parents' combined net income
2. Find the estimated cost of raising children at that income level (from state tables)
3. Each parent pays their proportional share

The parent with less parenting time typically makes child support payments to the other.

## What You Need to File

If you have minor children, your divorce filing must include:

- **Parenting Plan** — detailed schedule and decision-making allocation
- **Child Support Worksheet** — showing the calculation
- **Financial Affidavit** — income and expense disclosure
