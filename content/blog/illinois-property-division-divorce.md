One of the biggest concerns in any Illinois divorce is: who gets what? Illinois follows **equitable distribution** — which does not mean equal, but fair based on the circumstances.

## Marital vs. Separate Property

**Marital property** includes most assets and debts acquired during the marriage, regardless of whose name is on them:
- Family home
- Retirement accounts and pensions (the portion earned during marriage)
- Bank accounts and investments
- Vehicles
- Businesses started during marriage

**Separate property** generally stays with the original owner:
- Assets owned before the marriage
- Inheritances received by one spouse
- Gifts received by one spouse from a third party
- Assets explicitly excluded by a prenuptial agreement

The line can blur. Separate property that gets mixed with marital assets (called **commingling**) can become marital property.

## What "Equitable" Means in Practice

Illinois courts consider multiple factors when dividing marital property:

- Length of the marriage
- Each spouse's contribution to acquiring the property
- Each spouse's economic circumstances
- Whether either spouse dissipated (wasted) marital assets
- Tax consequences of the division
- Child custody arrangements

For most marriages, especially shorter ones with simpler finances, courts tend toward a roughly equal split — but it's never guaranteed.

## The Family Home

The home is often the most significant asset. Common outcomes:
- **One spouse buys out the other** and refinances the mortgage in their name only
- **The home is sold** and proceeds split
- **One spouse stays** (often the custodial parent) until children finish school, then the home is sold

## Retirement Accounts

Retirement accounts require a special court order called a **Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO)** to divide without tax penalties. This is separate from your divorce judgment and is often handled after the divorce is finalized.

## Creating Your Settlement Agreement

In an uncontested divorce, you and your spouse agree on how to divide everything and document it in a **Marital Settlement Agreement (MSA)**. This is a binding contract that becomes part of your divorce judgment.
