If you have minor children and are divorcing in Illinois, you must submit a **parenting plan** to the court. This is a detailed document specifying how parental responsibilities and parenting time will be allocated.

Illinois uses the term "parental responsibilities" rather than "custody" — the focus is on decision-making and parenting time, not ownership.

## What a Parenting Plan Must Include

Illinois law requires parenting plans to address:

**Allocation of parental responsibilities:**
- Who makes decisions about education
- Who makes decisions about healthcare
- Who makes decisions about religious upbringing
- Who makes decisions about extracurricular activities

**Parenting time schedule:**
- Regular weekly schedule (which parent has the child on which days)
- Holiday schedule (Thanksgiving, Christmas, spring break, summer)
- How vacations and travel are handled
- Protocol for schedule changes

**Communication:**
- How parents communicate about the child
- How the child communicates with each parent
- Notice requirements for travel

**Dispute resolution:**
- What happens if parents disagree on decisions
- Whether mediation is required before court intervention

## Common Parenting Time Arrangements

**Equal/50-50:** Week on/week off, or 2-2-3 rotation. Common when both parents live nearby and have similar schedules.

**Primary residence with visitation:** Child lives primarily with one parent; other parent has regular parenting time (typically every other weekend + one weeknight).

**Majority time:** 70/30 or similar split. Common when one parent has a more flexible schedule or lives closer to the child's school.

Illinois courts start from the presumption that both parents being involved is in the child's best interest — but the specific arrangement should reflect your family's actual circumstances.

## Creating Your Parenting Plan

Both parents can agree on a parenting plan and submit it to the court. In uncontested divorces, the agreed plan is almost always approved if it addresses all required elements.
