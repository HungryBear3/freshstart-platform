If you're going through a divorce in Illinois and you have children, you cannot finalize the divorce without a **parenting plan** — a written agreement that covers how you and your spouse will share parenting responsibilities after the divorce.

Illinois courts take parenting plans seriously. Vague or incomplete plans get rejected. Here's what yours must include.

## What Illinois Law Requires

Under the Illinois Marriage and Dissolution of Marriage Act (750 ILCS 5/602.10), a parenting plan must address:

1. Allocation of parental responsibilities (decision-making)
2. Parenting time schedule
3. How disputes between parents will be handled
4. Right of first refusal provisions (optional but recommended)
5. Holiday and vacation schedule
6. Transportation arrangements

Let's break each one down.

## 1. Allocation of Parental Responsibilities

Illinois replaced the old "custody" terminology with **allocation of parental responsibilities**, which covers significant decisions about your child's life in four areas:

- **Education** — school enrollment, educational decisions
- **Healthcare** — medical, dental, mental health decisions
- **Religion** — religious upbringing and activities
- **Extracurricular activities** — sports, clubs, lessons

Your parenting plan must specify whether each category is handled **jointly** (both parents decide together) or **solely** (one parent has final say). You can split these — for example, joint healthcare but sole education to one parent.

## 2. Parenting Time Schedule

The plan must include a detailed schedule specifying when the child is with each parent. This includes:

- **Regular weekly schedule** — which days/nights the child is with each parent on a typical week
- **Transition times** — exactly when exchanges happen and where
- **School year vs. summer** — many families have different arrangements during summer break

Be specific. "Alternating weekends" is not enough. Include start and end times.

## 3. Holiday and Special Days Schedule

The plan must address how you'll handle:

☐ Major holidays (Thanksgiving, Christmas/Hanukkah, New Year's, Easter/Passover)
☐ School breaks (spring break, winter break, summer)
☐ Birthdays — child's birthday, each parent's birthday
☐ Mother's Day / Father's Day
☐ Three-day weekends (Labor Day, Memorial Day, etc.)

A common approach: alternate major holidays each year (one parent gets Thanksgiving in odd years, the other in even years). Whatever you agree on, write it out explicitly.

## 4. Dispute Resolution

Illinois requires the plan to address how disputes between parents will be handled before going back to court. Common mechanisms:

- **Direct negotiation** — parents try to resolve disagreements themselves first
- **Mediation** — neutral third party helps reach agreement
- **Parenting coordinator** — appointed professional who makes binding decisions on minor disputes

You don't have to get creative here. A simple provision that disputes go to mediation before litigation is sufficient.

## 5. Right of First Refusal (Optional but Recommended)

This provision says that if one parent needs childcare for more than a certain number of hours (often 4 or 8), they must offer the other parent the opportunity to care for the child before using a third party.

It's optional under Illinois law but prevents disputes down the road and is seen favorably by courts.

## 6. Transportation

Specify who is responsible for transporting the child at each exchange — particularly important if parents live far apart. Address:

- Who drives to exchanges
- Where exchanges happen (neutral location, school, a parent's home)
- What happens if a parent is running late

## Child Support Is Separate

A parenting plan doesn't typically include child support numbers — that goes in the Marital Settlement Agreement. However, your parenting time arrangement directly affects the child support calculation under Illinois law. More parenting time generally means lower support obligations for the higher-earner.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Too vague.** "Reasonable parenting time" is not a plan. Illinois courts want specifics.

**Missing holidays.** Forgetting to address Thanksgiving or spring break means you'll be negotiating it every year.

**No transition logistics.** Failing to specify who does pickups and where creates endless friction.

**Ignoring long-distance provisions.** If there's any chance one parent may relocate, address relocation notice requirements.
