Child support is one of the most misunderstood parts of Illinois divorce. Many parents assume it's a fixed percentage of income — it's not. Illinois uses an **income shares model** that considers both parents' incomes and the actual cost of raising children.

## How Illinois Child Support Is Calculated

Illinois uses guidelines established under the Illinois Marriage and Dissolution of Marriage Act (IMDMA). The calculation involves:

1. **Both parents' net incomes** — after taxes, Social Security, and certain deductions
2. **The number of children**
3. **The parenting time split** — more parenting time with one parent changes the calculation
4. **Extraordinary expenses** — health insurance premiums, childcare, special needs

The state publishes an **Income Shares Schedule** that estimates the total cost of raising children at various combined income levels. That cost is then split proportionally between the parents based on their incomes.

## Example Calculation

Parent A earns $5,000/month net. Parent B earns $3,000/month net.
Combined income: $8,000/month.

For one child, the Income Shares Schedule might estimate the total child-rearing cost at $1,400/month.

Parent A's share: 62.5% ($875/month)
Parent B's share: 37.5% ($525/month)

If Parent B has primary custody, Parent A pays $875/month in child support.

## What Reduces Child Support

**Parenting time adjustment:** If the paying parent has the child 40%+ of nights per year (146+ nights), a parenting time credit reduces their obligation.

**Additional expenses:** If one parent pays health insurance premiums for the child, that cost is often credited against their support obligation.

## Deviating From the Guidelines

Judges can deviate from guideline amounts if they find it would be unjust. Common reasons include:

- Extraordinary medical needs
- Educational expenses
- Very high or very low income situations
- Agreement between both parents
