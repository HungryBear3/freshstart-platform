One of the most confusing parts of filing for divorce in Illinois is figuring out which forms you actually need. The Illinois court system has dozens of forms — but most people only need a handful. Here's a clear breakdown.

## The Core Forms

### 1. Petition for Dissolution of Marriage
This is your main filing document. It tells the court who you are, who your spouse is, when you got married, and what you're asking for (property division, custody, etc.). Every divorce starts here.

### 2. Summons
The summons is served on your spouse along with the Petition. It officially notifies them that a divorce case has been filed and gives them a deadline to respond (30 days).

### 3. Appearance and Waiver (for uncontested divorces)
If your spouse agrees to the divorce and doesn't want to contest anything, they sign this form instead of filing a formal response. It speeds things up considerably.

### 4. Marital Settlement Agreement
This is the agreement you and your spouse reach about dividing property, debts, and (if applicable) custody and support. It gets signed by both parties and submitted to the court.

### 5. Judgment for Dissolution of Marriage
This is the final order the judge signs to make your divorce official. Your attorney or the court typically prepares this based on your settlement agreement.

## If You Have Children

Additional forms are required when minor children are involved:

- **Allocation of Parental Responsibilities** — outlines parenting time and decision-making
- **Child Support Worksheets** — calculates support based on Illinois income shares guidelines
- **Parenting Plan** — detailed schedule for custody and visitation

## County-Specific Forms

Illinois has 102 counties and each circuit court has slightly different local forms. Cook County uses the CCDR series. DuPage, Kane, and Will counties have their own versions. Always check your local circuit court's website for the exact forms required.

## What Happens If You Use the Wrong Form?

Your case can be delayed or rejected by the clerk's office. Judges can also reject agreements that don't meet Illinois legal requirements. Getting the forms right the first time saves weeks.
