Filing for divorce in Illinois doesn't have to mean hiring an expensive attorney. If you and your spouse agree on the major issues — property, debts, and children — you can often handle the process yourself. Here's what you need to know.

## Step 1: Meet the Residency Requirement

At least one spouse must have lived in Illinois for 90 days before filing. If you haven't hit that mark yet, you'll need to wait.

## Step 2: Grounds for Divorce

Illinois is a no-fault divorce state. You only need to state that "irreconcilable differences" have caused the breakdown of the marriage. You don't need to prove fault or blame.

## Step 3: Gather Your Documents

Before you file, collect:
- Marriage certificate
- Financial records (bank accounts, debts, property)
- If children are involved: information about custody and support

## Step 4: Complete the Required Forms

Illinois requires several forms to start the divorce process:
- **Petition for Dissolution of Marriage** — the main filing document
- **Summons** — notifies your spouse of the filing
- **CCDR forms** — used in Cook County and other circuits

The specific forms vary by county. Fresh Start IL handles the paperwork for you so nothing gets missed.

## Step 5: File with the Circuit Court

File your completed forms at the Circuit Court Clerk's office in the county where either spouse lives. Filing fees typically range from $200–$350 depending on the county.

## Step 6: Serve Your Spouse

Your spouse must be formally served with divorce papers. This can be done by a process server, sheriff, or by waiver if your spouse agrees to sign an acceptance of service.

## Step 7: Wait for the Response

Your spouse has 30 days to respond. In uncontested cases, they typically sign a waiver of service and the case moves forward quickly.

## Step 8: Final Hearing

In uncontested divorces, the final hearing is usually brief — often less than 10 minutes. A judge reviews your paperwork and enters the divorce decree.

## How Long Does It Take?

Illinois has a mandatory waiting period of 6 months of separation, but judges can waive this in uncontested cases. Most uncontested divorces are finalized within 2–4 months of filing.
